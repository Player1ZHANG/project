II. — Chapter 02 —

A short introduction

I'm a designer working between research, fabrication, and emerging tech. The goal isn't to be a generalist — it's to make sure each side keeps the other honest. Engineering on its own gets clever and forgets why; concept on its own gets earnest and forgets how.

Currently completing my Master of Design at UC Berkeley, where my work moves between accessibility, multi-sensory interfaces, and speculative future systems. Before Berkeley, four years of Product Design at Tongji University in Shanghai — industrial design, critical making, and installation work that sat halfway between gallery and laboratory.

— Case 01 / 04 · 2022 · Smart Medical Device · Solo —

AI+Acupuncture

TCM in a feedback loop with AI.

A smart medical device that uses EMG sensing and computer vision to detect the "de-qi" sensation — and adjust treatment in real time.

Traditional Chinese Medicine relies on the practitioner's ability to feel the moment when needle and meridian "click" — the de-qi response. This device makes that moment legible: surface EMG detects the muscle-twitch signature, a phone-mounted camera locks onto the patch position, and a low-power laser delivers the controlled stimulus.

The hardware splits into three calm parts — eight magnetic patches, a transparent PMMA charging base, and a phone that becomes the eyes of the system.

— Case 02 / 04 · 2022 · Critical Installation · Co-author —

Data Feeder

Feed your data twin.

A critical installation about giving up identity to algorithms — one piece of data at a time.

Visitors print their own personal data (fingerprint, face scan, voice sample) onto paper. They feed the paper to a machine. Behind a one-way mirror, their digital twin gradually brightens to life — in four stages, from clear reflection to a twin that breaks free and walks away.

The work didn't ask "what is a digital twin?" — it asked "would you feed yours?"

— Case 03 / 04 · 2023 · Speculative Wearable · Solo —

Sleepwalker Autopilot

A nightly guardian.

An autopilot for sleepwalkers — vision-guided hazard detection and a soft physical redirect, all while the wearer keeps sleeping.

Sleepwalking affects up to 4% of adults and the existing tools either wake the wearer (defeating the purpose of sleep) or do nothing at all. Sleepwalker Autopilot takes a different angle: vision-based hazard detection running continuously through the night, with a soft physical redirect — the wearer never wakes, but never reaches the stairs.

Inspired by the form language of bone and skeleton — protective without armouring, present without dominating. The form admits the body underneath.

— Case 04 / 04 · 2026 · Multi-modal Product · Team of 4 —

Emora

A companion for the blind community.

An AI accessibility companion that turns ambient surroundings into emotionally-aware, spoken descriptions.

Built around real Lighthouse community needs. The product combines computer vision, NLP, and a hardware device into a multi-modal tool — three modes (Free, Focus, Find) covering different blind-user contexts.

What separates Emora from typical accessibility tech: it doesn't just describe what's there. It translates the felt quality of a space — the warmth of light through a window, the energy of a crowd, the quiet of an empty room. Translation, not capture.
